Who is Rama?

Rama is a major deity in Hinduism and is considered the seventh avatar (incarnation) of the god Vishnu.

He is often depicted as a blue-skinned god with a bow and arrow and is revered as a divine king, a hero, and a symbol of duty and loyalty.

Life and Story

Rama was born in the city of Ayodhya to King Dasharatha and Queen Kausalya.

He was the eldest of four brothers, and his early life was marked by numerous challenges and adventures.

Rama was educated in the arts of war and governance by the sage Vasishtha.

He was also trained in the use of the bow and arrow by the sage Vishvamitra.

Marriage and Exile

Rama married Sita, the daughter of King Janaka, after winning her hand in a contest.

However, his happiness was short-lived, as he was exiled to the forest for 14 years by his father, King Dasharatha.

Rama's exile was a result of a conspiracy by his stepmother, Queen Kaikeyi, who wanted her son Bharata to become the king.

Battle with Ravana

Rama's wife, Sita, was abducted by the demon king Ravana, and Rama waged a war against him to rescue her.

With the help of his brother Lakshmana and the monkey god Hanuman, Rama defeated Ravana and rescued Sita.

The battle between Rama and Ravana is considered one of the most epic battles in Hindu mythology.

Significance and Worship

Rama is worshipped by millions of Hindus around the world, and his story is retold in the epic Ramayana.

He is often depicted in art and literature as a heroic figure, and his love for his wife Sita is legendary.

Rama's worship is an important part of Hinduism, and his temples can be found all over India and other parts of the world.

Teachings and Legacy

Rama's teachings emphasize the importance of duty, loyalty, and self-discipline.

He is considered a role model for Hindus, and his legacy continues to inspire and guide people around the world.

Rama's story has been retold and reinterpreted in many different ways over the centuries, and his impact on Hinduism and Indian culture cannot be overstated.
